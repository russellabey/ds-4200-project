Introduction

For over 60 years, smoking has been known to be linked to lung cancer, yet there are still millions of people who smoke. Lung cancer remains one of the most preventable yet deadliest cancers, driven not only by tobacco use but by a complex web of environmental exposures and individual health history.

By looking closely at the data, we wanted to move beyond the familiar warning and ask a more precise question: which specific factors, individually and in combination, are most associated with elevated lung cancer risk? Our project brings together clinical, behavioral, and environmental variables to give a fuller picture of lung cancer risk. Our hope is that the patterns we uncover can help people make more informed decisions about their own health.

To learn more about lung cancer research, you can check out reports from the American Association for Cancer Research e.g., https://aacrjournals.org/cebp/article/28/10/1563/71761/Cancer-Progress-and-Priorities-Lung-CancerCancer.

Variable name and details

Variable Name Details
lung_cancer_risk Binary value indicating if the patient has lung cancer (1) or not (0)
age Age
gender 1 - Male; 0 - Female
cigarettes_per_day Number of cigarettes smoked per day
alcohol_units_per_week Number of alcoholic units drank per week
air_pollution_index Air pollution index
bmi BMI
exercise_hours_per_week Number of hours spent exercising per week
diet_quality Range of 1 (poor diet) - 5 (balanced diet)
family_history_cancer Binary value indicating if the patient's family has a history of lung cancer (1) or not (0)
copd Binary value indicating if the patient has chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (1) or not (0)
asthma Binary value indicating if the patient has asthma (1) or not (0)
previous_tb Binary value indicating if the patient has had tuberculosis (1) or not (0)
