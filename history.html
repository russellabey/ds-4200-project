What if I quit today?

This interactive chart can help someone who is considering quitting to see the impact if they quit at the current moment. This can help someone who is unsure about the current and future risks of their smoking habits. With the sliders, someone can fit their habits and see how if they continue to smoke, the risks continue to escalate.

Conclusion

From the data, it is apparent that smoking is the number one factor in your control that increases the risk of lung cancer. While other aspects of life can also increase risk (alcohol, exercise, etc.), none are as impactful as cigarettes. Quitting is, by far, the best way to reduce your risk, so take your health into your own hands.

Other factors can also influence risk, mainly previous medical history. While this is not something in your control, simply being aware of the increased chance will benefit you. If you are in a higher-risk group and something does not feel right with your breathing, do not hesitate to get screened for cancer, or at the very least schedule a doctor’s appointment. The data tells a clear warning.

To further research factors that could increase risk of lung cancer, a possible route would be to look at grand environmental or societal factors. Closely examining air pollution index based on geographic location, income status, access to healthcare, and other factors with enormous impact on everyday life could yield interesting observations about how certain areas or people may be predisposed to lung cancer from birth.
